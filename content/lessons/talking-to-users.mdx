The single most common way founders waste a year is by "validating" an idea through conversations that were rigged from the start. You describe your brilliant idea, people are nice, they say it sounds great — and you hear validation where there was only politeness. The discipline that fixes this is captured in Rob Fitzpatrick's *The Mom Test.*

## The core idea

The Mom Test is a set of rules for talking to customers such that **even your mom can't lie to you** — because you never put her in a position to. The rule: **talk about their life and past behavior, never your idea.**

- Bad: "Would you use an app that helps you do X?" → invites a polite yes.
- Good: "Walk me through the last time you dealt with X. What did you do? What did it cost you?" → produces facts.

Opinions about the future are worthless; concrete stories about the past are gold. People are unreliable narrators of what they *would* do and reliable reporters of what they *have* done.

## Three rules

<Framework name="The Mom Test rules">
1. **Talk about their life, not your idea.** Your idea is yours to evaluate from their facts — don't outsource judgment to a polite stranger.
2. **Ask about specifics in the past, not generics in the future.** "Last time" beats "usually" beats "would you."
3. **Listen more, talk less.** If you're pitching, you've lost the interview.
</Framework>

## Compliments are fool's gold

The warmest sign in a bad interview is a compliment — "I love this, you should totally build it!" It feels like validation and is worth nothing. What you're hunting for is **commitment**: real currency the person spends to show they mean it.

<Pull>Anything they give up that has value — time, money, or reputation — is signal. A compliment costs them nothing, so it tells you nothing.</Pull>

Commitments look like: agreeing to a real next meeting, introducing you to their boss, pre-paying a deposit, giving you their data, or piloting your prototype. "That's interesting" is not a commitment. "Can you send it to me Monday so I can show my team?" is.

## Running a good conversation

Keep it casual, not a formal "interview." Get them telling stories about how the problem actually shows up in their week. Dig into the *workaround* they currently use — its existence (or absence) tells you whether the pain is real. End by asking for a commitment as a forcing function: if they won't give up anything, the enthusiasm was fluff.

Do this for the riskiest assumption from your idea scorecard *before* you build. Five honest Mom Test conversations will save you six months of building the wrong thing.
