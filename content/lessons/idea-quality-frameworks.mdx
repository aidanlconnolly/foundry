Most idea evaluation is vibes: "I'd use this," a few friends nodding, a TAM slide with a trillion-dollar number. That's how smart people talk themselves into mediocre ideas. The fix is to make the evaluation *structured and adversarial* — to interrogate an idea on the dimensions that actually predict whether a fast-growing company can be built on top of it.

Here are the five that matter most. Score your idea honestly on each; the lowest scores are your real risks.

## 1. Problem severity — painkiller or vitamin?

The single best predictor of early traction is **how badly it hurts.** A *painkiller* solves an acute, expensive, recurring problem; people will rearrange their budget to make it stop. A *vitamin* is a nice-to-have — genuinely pleasant, easy to admire in an interview, and quietly fatal, because admiration doesn't convert and it doesn't retain.

The tell is emotional and behavioral: for a painkiller, people are already spending money, time, or duct-tape on a workaround *today*. If nobody has bothered to hack together a solution, the pain probably isn't severe enough yet.

## 2. Frequency — how often does it bite?

Severity times frequency is roughly the size of the wound you're treating. A painful problem that occurs once every five years (say, buying a house) builds a very different company than one that bites daily (communication, payments, dev tooling). High frequency gives you more shots at habit formation, retention, and word-of-mouth. Low frequency forces you to nail acquisition economics because you rarely get a second at-bat.

## 3. Market size — TAM, SAM, SOM (done honestly)

Marc Andreessen's famous claim is that **the market is the thing that matters most** — a great market will pull a product out of a mediocre team, while a great team can't manufacture a market that isn't there. So size it, but size it bottoms-up:

<Framework name="TAM / SAM / SOM">
- **TAM** — *Total Addressable Market*: all the money spent on this problem if you owned 100% of it.
- **SAM** — *Serviceable Available Market*: the slice your product and business model could actually serve.
- **SOM** — *Serviceable Obtainable Market*: what you can realistically capture in the next few years.

Build TAM from the **bottom up** (number of customers × realistic annual spend), not top-down from an analyst's trillion-dollar headline. A credible $2B bottoms-up TAM beats a hand-wavy $80B one every time you're in front of a real investor.
</Framework>

## 4. Why now — the timing window

Most good ideas have been thought of before. The interesting question is *why this is finally possible or urgent today.* A sharp **"why now"** points to a specific recent shift:

- **Technological** — capable LLMs made vertical-AI products buildable that weren't two years ago.
- **Regulatory** — a new law forces buyers to change behavior (open banking, e-invoicing mandates).
- **Behavioral** — a durable shift in how people work or buy (remote-first, creator economy).

If your honest "why now" is "I finally have time," the window may not actually be open — and you'll be early, which is indistinguishable from wrong for a long, expensive time.

## 5. Founder–market fit — your unfair advantage

Why *you*? Founder–market fit is the specific, hard-to-copy edge you bring: deep domain scar tissue, a distribution channel you already own, or credibility that opens doors a generic team can't. It doesn't have to be technical. A consultant who has watched the same broken process at fifteen clients has a real edge — *if* the edge is insight or access, not just familiarity.

## Avoiding the classic traps

Three idea shapes look great and usually aren't:

- **Tarpit ideas** — perennially appealing problems (a better events app, a friend-finder) that attract endless founders and kill them slowly, because the reason they're unsolved is structural, not for lack of trying.
- **Fake markets** — markets that exist in a deck but not in a budget; nobody actually has a line item for it.
- **Solution-in-search-of-a-problem** — you fell in love with a technology (yours or AI's) and went hunting for somewhere to apply it.

<Callout title="How to use the five dimensions">
Don't average them into a single score and call it a day. Treat the framework as a **risk finder**: your lowest score is the assumption most likely to kill you, and therefore the thing to test *first* in customer discovery. Idea quality isn't a gate you pass once — it's a hypothesis you go validate in the next stage.
</Callout>

The point of all this isn't to find a "perfect" idea — those don't survive contact with reality anyway. It's to know, before you spend a year of your life, *which assumption is load-bearing* so you can go break it cheaply.
