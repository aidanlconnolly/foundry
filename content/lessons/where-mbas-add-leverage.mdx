A Wharton MBA with consulting experience brings real, valuable skills to a startup — and a few specific failure modes. Knowing exactly where you add leverage (and where you don't) lets you build a team that compounds your strengths instead of duplicating them.

## Where you genuinely add value

<Framework name="High-leverage zones for business founders">
- **Customer development.** You're comfortable getting in front of people, asking sharp questions, and synthesizing — exactly what discovery needs.
- **Fundraising narrative.** Structuring a story, a model, and a deck, and running a tight process, is squarely in your wheelhouse.
- **Go-to-market.** Choosing a motion, building the funnel, early sales — business founders often out-execute technical ones here.
- **Operating rigor.** Hiring, finance, partnerships, prioritization, and turning chaos into a functioning company.
- **Strategic judgment.** Pattern-matching across markets and competitors to make sharp calls.
</Framework>

These are not trivial — many brilliant technical founders struggle exactly here, which is why the **business + technical pair** is such a durable model. Your job is to be the engine for customers, capital, and operations.

## Where you can get in the way

The same training that helps can hurt if unchecked:

- **Analysis paralysis.** The instinct to research, model, and plan can substitute for the thing that actually matters early: building and talking to users. A 40-slide market analysis is not progress; a shipped MVP and ten user conversations are.
- **Deck-thinking.** Treating a beautiful plan as the deliverable. Startups reward shipped reality over polished intention.
- **Faking the technical role.** Pretending to be the CTO because you can read a tutorial. Respect the depth of real engineering; partner for it.
- **Status-seeking.** Optimizing for what looks impressive (big titles, big hires, PR) over what creates value (retention, revenue).

<Pull>Your consulting reflex says "analyze until certain." The startup reflex is "ship to learn." Rewire toward action.</Pull>

## Play to strength, staff the gaps

The winning move is honest self-assessment: lean hard into your genuine advantages — your network, your industry knowledge, your operating ability, your fundraising skill — and **build the team around your gaps**, which for most business founders means a committed technical builder. Don't dilute your edge by spreading thin across things others do better; concentrate on being the best possible customer-and-capital-and-operations founder, and partner with someone who's the best possible builder.

Used well, the MBA/consulting background is a real asset — especially for the unglamorous operational grind of turning a product into a company. The trick is to deploy it as *leverage* on action, not as a substitute for it.
