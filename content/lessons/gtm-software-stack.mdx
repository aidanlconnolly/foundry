A small team can run a sophisticated go-to-market with a handful of tools — but the trick is adopting them in the right order. Buy too much too early and you'll spend weeks configuring enterprise software for customers you don't have; buy too little and you'll fly blind. Here's a sane progression.

## Start with measurement

Before optimizing anything, you need to *see* what's happening. The first and most important GTM tool is **product analytics**:

- **PostHog** (a great all-in-one default — analytics, session replay, feature flags), or **Amplitude / Mixpanel**.
- Define your **activation event** (the moment a user first gets value) and your **north-star**, and instrument them.

You can't improve activation or retention you can't see. This comes *before* a CRM, before paid ads, before automation.

## The stack by stage

<Framework name="GTM stack progression">
- **MVP / first users:** Product analytics (PostHog). A spreadsheet or a lightweight CRM (**Attio**) for the handful of leads you're working by hand.
- **First revenue / repeatable:** A real but light **CRM** (Attio, HubSpot starter), **lifecycle messaging** (Customer.io) to automate onboarding/re-engagement, and a marketing site (**Webflow/Framer**).
- **Scaling:** A **CDP** (Segment) to route data cleanly, fuller marketing automation (HubSpot), and **attribution** to understand which channels actually drive revenue.
</Framework>

## Don't out-tool your stage

<Pull>The point of the GTM stack is leverage — letting a tiny team do what used to need a department. But every tool is also setup cost, a subscription, and a thing to maintain. Add each one only when a real need pulls it in.</Pull>

The anti-pattern is a pre-revenue startup with Salesforce, a marketing automation suite, and an attribution platform — thousands of dollars and dozens of hours configuring machinery for a scale they're nowhere near. Start with analytics and a lightweight CRM; let growth pull in the heavier tooling exactly when a bottleneck demands it.

## The leverage principle

The modern GTM stack is genuinely powerful: lifecycle automation, self-serve analytics, and AI-assisted tooling let two people run growth that once required ten. Use that leverage — but sequence it. **Instrument first** (so you're deciding on data), automate the repetitive parts as patterns emerge, and reserve the heavy enterprise tooling for when you're actually scaling a proven motion.
