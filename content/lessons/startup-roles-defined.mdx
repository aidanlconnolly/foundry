"Team" is vague; roles are concrete. Knowing precisely what each early role does — and what it doesn't — lets you see your gaps, hire in the right order, and avoid the classic mistake of cloning yourself or hiring a title you don't yet need.

## The early roles

<Framework name="Who's who in an early team">
- **Founder / CEO** — Owns vision, fundraising, recruiting, culture, and the highest-leverage customer and strategy calls. Early on, also does whatever's on fire. The CEO's real product is *the company*.
- **Co-founder / CTO** — Peer owner leading product and engineering technically. Shares founder equity, risk, and decisions.
- **Founding engineer** — Employee #1–5 who builds heavily and shapes architecture. Meaningful equity, but an employee, not an owner.
- **Founding designer** — Owns product and brand design where craft differentiates (think Airbnb, Notion, Figma). Often as pivotal as the first engineer.
- **First GTM hire** — The first dedicated sales/growth person, added *after* founders have personally proven a repeatable way to acquire customers.
- **First ops / generalist** — The "chief of stuff" who absorbs finance, recruiting ops, and the long tail so founders stay on leverage.
- **The "first 10"** — The earliest cohort who collectively set culture, the talent bar, and core capabilities.
</Framework>

## The CEO's job is not "everything"

A trap for capable founders — especially ex-consultants who can do many things competently — is to keep doing all of them. Early, you do wear every hat. But the CEO's *durable* job is narrow and non-delegable: **set direction, raise money, recruit great people, and make the few highest-stakes calls.** Everything you can hand off, you eventually should — your scarcest resource is attention on the things only you can do.

## Why the first 10 matter so much

<Pull>Your first ten hires don't just do work — they define what "good" means, who you'll attract next, and the culture that becomes nearly impossible to change later.</Pull>

A-players attract A-players; a single mediocre early hire lowers the bar and repels the best future candidates. This is why founders should over-invest in early recruiting and be slow to settle. The cost of a bad early hire isn't one salary — it's the compounding drag on everything and everyone that follows.

## Use this as a diagnostic

Don't hire roles because startups "should" have them. Map your real gaps against this list and ask: *what missing capability is most blocking the next 90 days of progress?* That's your next co-founder or hire. Often the honest answer for a business-leaning founder is "someone who can build" — which is exactly why the technical co-founder question (last lesson) is so central. Roles are tools; add the one that unblocks the most.
