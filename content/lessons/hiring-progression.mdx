Hiring order is strategy. Hire the wrong role too early and you burn runway and add coordination overhead; hire too late and a bottleneck strangles growth. The discipline is to hire against **real, specific bottlenecks** — in roughly this progression.

## The typical sequence

<Framework name="Early hiring progression">
1. **Founding engineer(s)** — Once there's more to build than the founders can build, the first hires expand product velocity.
2. **Founding designer** (if craft differentiates) — Often alongside or just after the first engineer.
3. **First GTM hire** — *After* founders have personally proven a repeatable acquisition motion. You hire to *scale* sales, not to discover it.
4. **More builders / specialists** — Depth in the areas where the product is straining (data, infra, key features).
5. **First ops / generalist** — A "chief of stuff" to absorb finance, recruiting ops, and the long tail so founders stay on leverage.
6. **First managers / leads** — When a function is big enough that *coordination* is the bottleneck, not just doing the work.
</Framework>

## The cardinal rule: founders do it first

Before you hire someone to own a function, **do it yourself long enough to understand it.** This is most important for sales:

<Pull>Founders must crack a repeatable way to win customers before hiring a salesperson. If you hire sales to *find* product-market fit, you'll just pay someone to fail at the thing only you can figure out.</Pull>

The same logic applies broadly — you can't hire well for a role whose success you can't yet define. Founder-led selling, founder-led support, founder-led recruiting early on isn't just frugality; it's how you learn what "good" looks like so you can later hire and manage for it.

## Hire against bottlenecks, not vanity

The unhealthy reasons to hire: a competitor staffed up, you have cash burning a hole, a bigger headcount "looks like a real company," or you think a startup is "supposed to" have a Head of X. The healthy reason: a **specific, persistent constraint** that a hire would relieve and that you can clearly articulate. If you can't name the bottleneck, you're not ready for that hire.

## When to add management

Management is itself a role you add deliberately — usually when a single function has grown past the point where one person (often a founder) can both do the work and coordinate everyone doing it. Adding managers too early creates hierarchy and politics in a company that should still be flat and fast; too late, and execution fractures as teams grow. Watch for the signal: when *coordination* — not capacity — becomes the constraint, it's time.

Stay lean as long as you honestly can. Every hire adds cost, communication overhead, and culture weight. The best early teams are smaller than outsiders expect, precisely because they hire only against real bottlenecks — and AI tooling now lets a small team do more before the next hire is truly necessary.
