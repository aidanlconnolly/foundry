There's no single right shape for a founding team, but there are well-worn patterns with known trade-offs — and one composition that shows up again and again among companies that work. Knowing the models lets you choose deliberately and see your model's failure mode before it bites.

## The models that have worked

<Framework name="Early operating models">
- **Solo founder + contractors.** One founder coordinating freelance help. Maximal control and equity; minimal committed talent. Works for simple products or as a bridge — but no in-house technical ownership and a single point of failure.
- **Two technical co-founders.** Two builders (think the Collison brothers, early Stripe). Ships fast and deep; risk is a gap on GTM/business until they hire for it.
- **Business + technical pair.** One builder, one seller/operator. Cleanly splits the two hardest early jobs. A classic, robust setup.
- **Hub-and-spoke.** A central founder with specialized spokes (early hires/contractors). Flexible, but knowledge and risk concentrate in the hub.
- **2–3 balanced co-founders.** A small founding team with at least one technical and clear role splits. The most common high-performer.
</Framework>

## The most common composition

If you study early teams that go the distance, the recurring shape is **2–3 co-founders, with 1–2 of them technical.** There are good reasons:

- **2–3 is the sweet spot.** Enough to cover building and go-to-market and survive the workload; few enough to move fast and split equity without chaos. Solo is lonely and fragile under load; four-plus founders dilutes ownership and slows decisions.
- **At least one technical.** Software companies need committed, in-house technical ownership — not rented capacity — especially as the product gets hard.
- **Complementary, not redundant.** The best pairs/trios cover different bases (build, sell, operate) rather than three people who all do the same thing.

<Pull>Two to three founders, at least one who can build, with complementary skills and aligned values — that's the boring, reliable shape of teams that make it.</Pull>

## Choose deliberately, name the failure mode

Each model has a predictable weak spot: solo+contractors lacks technical ownership; two-technical lacks early GTM; hub-and-spoke over-concentrates the hub. Pick the model that fits your reality, then **proactively patch its known gap** — e.g., a two-technical team prioritizing a GTM-minded third founder or early hire, or a solo non-technical founder treating "find a technical co-founder" as job #1.

For a Wharton/consulting founder specifically: you're often the *business* side of a business+technical pair. The highest-leverage move is usually to find the builder — and to be honest that your value is in customers, capital, and operating clarity, not in pretending to be the CTO.
