Different stages demand different scoreboards. Tracking the wrong metric — or worse, a vanity metric — leads you to optimize the wrong thing. Here's what actually matters, and roughly when.

## Pick a north-star

Above all the specific metrics sits one: the **north-star metric** — the single number that best captures the value you deliver (Airbnb: nights booked; Slack: messages sent; a SaaS tool: weekly active teams completing the core action). A good north-star is *customer value*, not vanity (not "signups" or "page views"), and it predicts sustainable growth. Choose one and let it focus the team.

## Metrics by stage

<Framework name="What to watch, when">
- **Pre-PMF (earliest):** **Retention & engagement.** Do people come back and reach the core value? Activation rate. This is the truth serum — nothing else matters if this is broken.
- **Early traction:** Growth rate (week-over-week early; month-over-month later), retention curves by cohort, and the first **unit economics**.
- **Scaling / fundraising:** **CAC, LTV, payback period, net revenue retention, ARR growth, gross margin.** Investors live here.
- **Always, from day one:** **Burn and runway.** The number that determines whether you survive.
</Framework>

## The unit-economics trio

As you grow, three linked numbers decide whether growth is healthy or a money furnace:

- **CAC** — fully-loaded cost to acquire a customer.
- **LTV** — total margin a customer generates over their lifetime.
- **Payback period** — months to recoup CAC.

Rules of thumb (model-dependent): **LTV:CAC ≥ 3** and **CAC payback < ~12 months.** If you're spending $300 to acquire a customer worth $200, growth makes you *poorer* — scaling that is a classic way to die with great-looking top-line numbers.

## Burn, runway, and default-alive

The most important founder number is **runway** = cash ÷ monthly burn = months until you're out of money. From it comes Paul Graham's crucial framing: are you **default alive or default dead?** Default-alive means your current cash *and* growth trajectory would reach profitability before the money runs out — you don't *need* another round to survive. Default-dead means you do.

<Pull>Know your burn and runway cold, and know whether you're default-alive. A founder who can't answer those instantly is flying blind.</Pull>

Being default-alive (or close) is strategic power: it means you raise from strength, not desperation — which, in the bifurcated 2026 market where the middle is squeezed, is exactly the position you want to be in.
