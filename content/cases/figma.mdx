## Snapshot

Figma was founded in **2012** by **Dylan Field** and **Evan Wallace**, who met as computer-science students at **Brown**. Field left school on a **Thiel Fellowship** ($100K to drop out and build). They spent **four years** in heads-down development before launching publicly in **2016**, then redefined product design as a collaborative, browser-based activity.

## The Origin

The seed was a technology, not a market. In **April 2011** Field became fascinated by **WebGL**; Wallace showed him you could re-implement serious image processing in the browser. For a year they explored what powerful creative tools running in a browser could become — eventually committing to design software, the domain where collaboration was most broken.

## The Insight

Design lived in heavyweight desktop apps, files were emailed around, and collaboration was a mess of versioned exports. Figma's bet: rebuild a professional design tool **in the browser** so that, like Google Docs, multiple people could edit the same file in real time, comment in context, and share with a link. "Google Docs for design."

## What They Built

A full vector design tool that runs in a browser tab with multiplayer editing. The deliberately *hard* part — and what they refused to compromise — was performance: making complex design files render and edit smoothly in a browser meant solving deep technical problems with WebGL and (later) WebAssembly, rather than shipping a thin web wrapper.

## The Build

Two technical founders and a small team spent **four years before public release** — an enormous, risky runway of burn with no revenue. That patience let them solve the rendering and real-time-collaboration problems *before* scaling distribution, so the product worked under load when it finally arrived. Evan Wallace's engineering on the browser graphics stack was foundational (he later built esbuild).

## Design

Beyond the product's own polish, the strategic design choice was **collaboration as the core primitive**: shared cursors, live multiplayer, commenting, and link-sharing weren't features bolted on — they were the reason to switch. That turned a solo creative tool into a team workflow.

## Go-To-Market

Figma launched **free** in 2016 and spread through designers worldwide, then teams. Its viral loop was structural: sharing a file via link exposed non-users to the product, and real-time collaboration pulled whole design teams (and their PM and engineering partners) onto the platform — classic product-led growth.

## The Money

- **~2012** — Field's **Thiel Fellowship** ($100K) funds the leap.
- **2013 onward** — Venture rounds from **Index, Greylock, Sequoia, Andreessen Horowitz, Kleiner Perkins** as the product matured.
- **Sept 2022** — **Adobe agrees to acquire Figma for ~$20B**.
- **Dec 18, 2023** — The deal is **terminated** under EU/UK antitrust pressure; Adobe pays a **$1B breakup fee**.
- **2025** — Figma **goes public** independently.

## Key Decisions

- **A long pre-launch runway.** Four years before release was a bet that solving the hard technical problems first would pay off — and it did.
- **Collaboration as the wedge.** Choosing real-time multiplayer as the core, not a feature, made Figma sticky and viral.
- **Stay independent.** When regulators blocked the $20B Adobe deal, Figma kept building and ultimately went public on its own terms.

## What To Steal

- A new platform capability (here, WebGL/the browser) can make an old product category newly winnable — that's a powerful "why now."
- Patience to solve the hardest technical problem first can be a moat competitors can't quickly copy.
- Make collaboration/sharing the core loop and growth can be structural rather than bought.
