Venture capital is the loudest path, but it's far from the only one — and for many great companies it's the wrong one. VC is fuel calibrated for a specific game (huge, fast outcomes). If that's not your game, these alternatives may fit your ambition and economics far better.

## The alternatives

<Framework name="Ways to fund a company">
- **Bootstrapping.** Grow from revenue and savings, no outside equity. You keep ownership and control and stay disciplined; you trade away speed and big up-front bets. Many wonderful, profitable companies never raise a dollar.
- **Angels.** Individual investors writing small early checks, often with useful expertise and networks. Common at pre-seed, frequently via SAFEs.
- **Accelerators (Y Combinator et al.).** A cohort program: modest capital + intense mentorship, a powerful peer network, and a demo day that opens investor doors. An excellent on-ramp, especially pre-seed.
- **Revenue-based financing.** Non-dilutive capital repaid as a percentage of revenue. Good for businesses with predictable revenue that don't want to sell equity.
- **Grants.** Non-dilutive funding from governments, foundations, or programs (and, for students, university funds like Wharton's Venture Lab Innovation Fund and venture awards).
- **ETA / search funds.** Raise capital to *buy and operate* an existing business instead of building from scratch — a lower-variance path with its own thriving ecosystem (and a Wharton specialty via the Perlman ETA Fellowship).
</Framework>

## Should you even raise VC?

The honest first question is the one from "How VC actually works": *could this plausibly become a $1B+ company?* If yes, and you want to play the high-growth game, VC can be rocket fuel. If not — or if you'd rather own more of a smaller, profitable company — raising venture money can saddle you with growth expectations your market can't support and a board that needs an outcome you can't deliver.

<Pull>VC isn't a prize you win for being a good company. It's a specific tool for a specific kind of company. Choosing *not* to raise can be the smarter, more lucrative decision.</Pull>

## Mix and match

These aren't mutually exclusive. A common modern path: do an **accelerator** for the network and mentorship, raise a small **angel/SAFE** seed, stay **capital-efficient** (and default-alive), and only raise institutional VC if and when a genuine high-growth opportunity demands it. Bootstrapping to revenue first can even put you in a *stronger* position to raise later — on your terms, from strength.

For a Wharton founder specifically, two paths deserve a hard look that the standard "raise VC" narrative ignores: **bootstrapping to a profitable business you own outright**, and **ETA** — buying and running an existing company, which the school actively supports and funds. The best funding path is the one that matches the company you actually want to build, not the one with the most press.
