Once you've found product-market fit and proven a working model, growth rounds (Series B and beyond) change both the trajectory and the nature of the company. Understanding what this capital is *for* — and what it does to you — helps you decide whether and how to take it.

## What growth capital is for

<Pull>Seed and A capital help you find and prove a model. Series B+ capital pours fuel on a model that already works — it amplifies an engine, it doesn't build one.</Pull>

Growth money funds *scaling the proven*: entering new markets, building out the sales and marketing org, expanding the product line, and accelerating ahead of competitors. The prerequisite is that the engine works — repeatable, efficient growth with sound unit economics. Raising a big round to *find* that engine is how companies blow up spectacularly: lots of fuel, no working motor.

## How growth changes the company

More capital and scale fundamentally shift how the company operates:

<Framework name="What changes at growth stage">
- **More stakeholders.** Bigger boards, more investors, and the beginnings of governance. Decisions involve more people.
- **More structure.** The org, process, and management layers from the last lesson become unavoidable.
- **Higher expectations.** You've taken money at a valuation you now have to *grow into* — the performance bar ratchets up.
- **Less nimbleness.** Inevitably, a 200-person company can't pivot like a 5-person one. Protecting speed becomes a deliberate effort, not a default.
</Framework>

## The valuation trap

A specific growth-stage danger: raising too large a round at too high a valuation. It feels like a win, but it sets a **bar you must grow into.** If you raised at a frothy peak and growth slows, your next round may be a **down round** — lower valuation than before — which is dilutive, bad for morale, and a negative signal. In the volatile 2026 market, where capital is concentrated and the middle is unforgiving, the discipline of *not over-raising* — taking what you can efficiently deploy at a valuation you can grow into — is more valuable than the biggest possible headline.

## The founder's choice

Not every company should chase growth rounds. Some are better as efficient, profitable, independent businesses (or modest acquisitions). The decision to step onto the growth-capital escalator is also a decision about the *kind of company and life* you're signing up for — bigger, faster, higher-stakes, less personally controllable. Make it deliberately, knowing that each round both expands what's possible and narrows your options to "keep growing fast." Growth capital is powerful fuel; point it at an engine that genuinely works, and only take as much as you can efficiently burn.
