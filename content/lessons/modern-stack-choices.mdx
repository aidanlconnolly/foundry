Once you've validated enough to build, the first real decision is *how.* Get this wrong and you either over-engineer (months building infrastructure for users you don't have) or under-build (a no-code prototype that can't become the real thing). The modern answer is more flexible than it's ever been.

## Three build paths

<Framework name="How to build v1">
- **No-code / low-code** (Webflow, Bubble, Airtable, Zapier, Softr) — Fastest to test standard workflows. Great for marketplaces' early days, internal tools, and concierge automation. Strains when you need custom logic, fine-grained UX, or scale.
- **AI-assisted custom** (Cursor, Claude Code, v0 on Next.js/Supabase/Vercel) — The 2026 default. A small team — even a non-traditional builder — can ship real, ownable custom software in days. Best balance of speed and control.
- **Hire it out** (agency or contractor) — Buys capacity if you truly can't build, but you lose iteration speed and own less knowledge. Usually a last resort for the core product.
</Framework>

## The principle: optimize for *your* iteration speed

The right stack is the one that lets **you** change the product daily without waiting on anyone. Early on, learning velocity beats everything — architecture purity, scalability, even code quality. A founder who can tweak the product the moment they learn something from a user will out-iterate a "perfect" stack that requires a ticket and a sprint.

That's why the AI-native path has become the default: it gives a tiny team the ability to build *and own* custom software at no-code speed. You're no longer forced to choose between "fast but throwaway" and "real but slow."

## Avoid premature scaling

The classic over-engineering trap: a pre-PMF team building microservices, Kubernetes, multi-region databases, and elaborate abstractions for a scale they're nowhere near. Every hour spent on infrastructure you don't need is an hour not spent finding product-market fit — your only real job at this stage. **Build the boring, monolithic, "good enough" version**, ship it, and let real load tell you what to harden. Most startups die from lack of demand, not from a database that couldn't scale.

## The AI-native default stack (2026)

A common, sane starting point: **Next.js + TypeScript** on **Vercel**, a managed Postgres (**Supabase**, **Neon**) or libSQL (**Turso**), **Stripe** for payments, and **Cursor / Claude Code** to build it. It's cheap, fast to deploy, scales surprisingly far, and — crucially — lets one founder move at the speed of their own learning. Reach for heavier tooling only when a real constraint forces it.
