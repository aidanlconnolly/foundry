Some ideas are dangerous precisely *because* they look good. They pass the dinner-party test, generate polite enthusiasm, and quietly waste years. Learning to spot these patterns is as valuable as finding good ideas.

## Vitamins vs. painkillers

The most common trap. A **vitamin** is a nice-to-have — pleasant, easy to nod along with in an interview, and quietly fatal because admiration doesn't convert to payment or retention. A **painkiller** solves an acute, recurring problem people will pay to make stop. The tell: for a painkiller, people are *already* spending money or duct-tape on a workaround. If no one's bothered to hack a solution, the pain probably isn't severe enough to build a company on.

## Tarpit ideas

**Tarpits** are problems that look obvious and appealing — a better events app, a way to make friends as an adult, a tool to coordinate group plans — and that attract a fresh wave of founders every year who almost all fail. The seductive part is that they seem *unsolved*; the deadly part is that they're unsolved for **structural reasons** (no retention, no willingness to pay, impossible cold-start), not because no one's tried. The diagnostic question:

<Pull>If this is such a good idea, why hasn't it worked yet? If your answer is "no one thought of it," you're almost certainly in a tarpit.</Pull>

## Fake markets

A **fake market** exists in surveys, decks, and conversation — but not in budgets. People say they'd love it; nobody has a line item or the urgency to actually buy. You confirm a fake market the hard way: lots of "this is great!" and zero conversions. The fix is to test for *money and urgency early*, not just interest — a pre-sale, a letter of intent, a deposit.

<Framework name="The four-question idea screen">
1. **Painkiller?** Are people already paying or hacking a workaround for this pain?
2. **Tarpit?** If it's unsolved, what's the *structural* reason — and have you really beaten it?
3. **Fake market?** Is there budget and urgency, or just enthusiasm?
4. **Solution-in-search-of-a-problem?** Did you start from the problem, or from a technology you wanted to use?
</Framework>

## The meta-lesson

None of these traps are obvious from the inside — that's what makes them traps. The defense isn't cleverness, it's **adversarial honesty**: actively look for the reason your idea is bad, test for money rather than smiles, and be willing to kill a beautiful idea that fails the screen. The cheapest pivot is the one you make before you've built anything.
