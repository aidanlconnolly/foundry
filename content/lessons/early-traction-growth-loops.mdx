Getting from zero to early traction is less about clever tactics and more about two things: doing unscalable work by hand to seed the first users, and engineering loops so that usage itself produces more usage. Master both and growth compounds; miss them and you're forever buying users one at a time.

## The "do things that don't scale" phase

Every great company started with founders doing manual, unscalable, slightly embarrassing work to win the first users:

- Airbnb's founders photographed listings door-to-door.
- Stripe's founders set up users' integrations by hand ("Collison installation").
- Countless founders DM'd their first hundred users individually.

<Pull>You are not too good to manually recruit, onboard, and delight your first users. That work is how you learn the business and earn the right to scale.</Pull>

This phase has two payoffs: it gets you real users *now*, and it teaches you — viscerally — what the product must do and why people would (or wouldn't) come back. Automating before you've done this by hand means automating guesses. Do it manually until you understand the work cold, *then* systematize.

## From funnel to loop

Most founders think in **funnels** — a one-way path from impression to customer that you must keep refilling from the top. Durable growth comes from **loops** — cycles where the output feeds back as input, so each cohort of users generates the next:

<Framework name="Growth loop types">
- **Viral / collaboration loop** — Using the product exposes it to others (Figma's shared files, Calendly invites, a doc you send). Growth is built into usage.
- **Content loop** — Users (or the product) create content that ranks/spreads and brings new users (Notion templates, UGC, programmatic SEO).
- **Referral loop** — Happy users invite others, often with an incentive (Dropbox's storage-for-referrals).
- **Paid loop** — Revenue from a customer funds acquiring the next, profitably (only a "loop" if unit economics work).
</Framework>

The magic of a loop is **compounding**: a funnel grows linearly with your effort; a loop grows on its own as the user base grows. Ask of your product: *what's the loop where using it creates more users?* If you can find and strengthen one, you have an engine instead of a treadmill.

## Sequencing

The order is: **hand-win the first users → observe what makes them stick and share → engineer that behavior into a loop → then scale the loop.** Don't pour money into acquisition before retention is real (you'll fill a leaky bucket), and don't expect a loop to appear without first understanding, by hand, why people value and spread the product. Traction is earned manually, then engineered to compound.
