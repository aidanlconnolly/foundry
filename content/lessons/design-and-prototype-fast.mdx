Design used to be a bottleneck — weeks of a designer's time before you could show anything. In 2026 it's the opposite: with Figma, Framer, and v0, you can turn an idea into a credible, clickable prototype in **hours to days**. That speed changes the validation game — you can put something tangible in front of users almost immediately and watch them react.

## Why prototype at all

Words are ambiguous; a screen is concrete. When you describe an idea, people imagine their own version and nod. When you show a prototype, they react to *your* version — and you instantly see where they're confused, where they light up, and where they reach for a button that isn't there. A prototype converts vague enthusiasm into specific, observable behavior. It's the bridge between Mom Test conversations and writing code.

## Match fidelity to the question

The most common waste is over-polishing. Fidelity should match what you're testing:

<Framework name="Fidelity ladder">
- **Sketch / wireframe** — testing *flow and structure*. Paper or low-fi Figma; minutes to make.
- **Clickable mid-fi** — testing *usability and comprehension*. Linked Figma frames; an afternoon.
- **High-fi / live** — testing *visual appeal and trust*, or for a pre-sale page. Framer or v0; a day.

If you're polishing pixels while still unsure people want the thing, you're optimizing the wrong variable.
</Framework>

## The modern toolkit

- **Figma** — the standard for UI design and clickable prototypes; real-time collaboration so a non-designer founder can work alongside a designer.
- **Framer** — design *and publish* a real, live marketing site or interactive prototype without code — perfect for a landing-page MVP.
- **v0** — describe a UI in plain language and get working React/Tailwind components; great for jumping from mockup to something that actually runs.
- **Claude / Cursor** — increasingly, you skip straight from prototype to a thin working app.

The point isn't tool mastery; it's that the cost of "show, don't tell" has collapsed. There's no excuse for validating with words when a clickable artifact takes an afternoon.

## What to do with it

Put the prototype in front of real prospective users (the same people from your Mom Test calls) and **watch silently**. Where do they pause? What do they try to click? What do they ignore? Resist explaining — if they're confused, that's data about the product, not a failure of your narration. Then iterate the prototype faster than you could ever iterate code, and only commit engineering once the design is provoking the reactions you want.
