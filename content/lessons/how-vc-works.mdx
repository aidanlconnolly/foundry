To raise venture capital well, you have to understand the machine you're plugging into — because almost every confusing thing a VC does (passing on profitable companies, pushing you to grow faster than feels safe, caring obsessively about market size) is rational once you see the economics driving them. VCs aren't being difficult. They're optimizing a very specific, very skewed payoff.

## Who's actually whose money is it?

A venture fund sits between two groups. **Limited Partners (LPs)** — pension funds, university endowments, foundations, family offices — are the people whose money it actually is. They commit capital to the fund. **General Partners (GPs)** are the VCs you meet: they raise the fund from LPs, deploy it into startups, take board seats, and try to return far more than they took in.

When a partner says "I love it but I need to check with the team," remember there's a *further* layer above them: the GPs have promised their LPs a return, typically over a **~10-year fund life**. Every check they write is a bet against that clock.

## How VCs get paid: 2 and 20

The classic structure is **"2 and 20."**

<Framework name="Fund economics (2 and 20)">
- **2% management fee**, charged annually on committed capital, funds salaries and operations. On a $200M fund that's ~$4M/year to run the firm. (In practice it often *tapers* in later years as deployment slows.)
- **20% carried interest** ("carry") is the GPs' share of the fund's *profits* — but only after LPs get their committed capital back. On a fund that turns $200M into $600M, the $400M profit is split 80/20: ~$320M to LPs, ~$80M carry to the GPs.

The implication: **the life-changing money for a VC is carry, not fees.** And carry only happens with big, profitable exits — which is why your individual ambition and theirs are (mostly) aligned.
</Framework>

## The power law — why "good" isn't good enough

Here's the fact that explains VC behavior more than any other. Venture returns follow a brutal **power law.** Harvard's Shikhar Ghosh found that roughly **75% of venture-backed companies never return investors' capital**, with 30–40% a total loss. Meanwhile the **top ~10% of investments generate 60–80% of all returns.**

<Pull>A venture portfolio isn't a basket of solid bets. It's a lottery where a tiny number of enormous winners pay for everything else — and the price of the ticket is that most of them go to zero.</Pull>

This is why a VC needs each new investment to have a *plausible path to returning the entire fund on its own.* If a partner runs a $300M fund, a company that might exit for $150M — a wonderful outcome for you — barely moves their number. They're not insulting your business when they pass; it's just "not venture-scale" *for that fund.* The same company might be perfect for a smaller seed fund, an angel, or no outside capital at all.

It also explains the push for growth. From inside the power law, a company that's "fine" is indistinguishable from a loss. VCs would rather you take a big swing that might 50× than play it safe for a 3×, because their model only works if the winners are gigantic. That incentive can be exactly right for you — or dangerously wrong, if your honest ceiling isn't venture-scale.

## What this means before you raise a dollar

Two practical takeaways:

1. **Ask the fund-returner question first.** Could this company plausibly be worth $1B+? If yes, venture fuel can be rocket fuel. If no, raising venture money may saddle you with growth expectations your market can't support — and a board that needs an outcome you can't deliver.
2. **Match the fund to the round.** A $500M fund can't get out of bed for a $1M seed check; a tiny pre-seed fund can't lead your Series B. Fund size dictates check size and the outcomes a VC needs — so part of fundraising is simply *targeting investors whose math your company can satisfy.*

<Callout title="The mental model to keep">
A VC is a fiduciary running a power-law portfolio against a 10-year clock, paid mostly through carry on a few giant winners. Hold that picture in your head and their questions stop feeling arbitrary: *How big can this get? How fast? Why you? Why now?* are not hoops — they're the VC checking whether you could be one of the handful of companies that makes their entire fund work.
</Callout>

You don't have to play this game. Plenty of great companies are built on revenue, angels, or a single small round. But if you *do* raise venture capital, raise it with your eyes open — knowing exactly what the person across the table needs your company to become.
