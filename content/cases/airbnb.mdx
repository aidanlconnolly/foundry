## Snapshot

Airbnb began in **2007–2008** when **Brian Chesky** and **Joe Gebbia** — two design-school friends who couldn't make rent — put air mattresses on their apartment floor and rented them to conference-goers. Engineer **Nathan Blecharczyk** joined as the third co-founder and CTO in **February 2008**. The company, first called **AirBed & Breakfast**, grew into a public hospitality giant.

## The Origin

The messy beginning is the whole legend: a San Francisco design conference had sold out the hotels, so Chesky and Gebbia offered "air bed and breakfast" to attendees. The site **airbedandbreakfast.com launched on August 11, 2008.** For more than a year it barely worked as a business — they relaunched it multiple times around events and were drowning in credit-card debt.

## The Insight

Strangers would pay to stay in other strangers' homes — *if* the experience felt safe, credible, and well-designed. The founders' design background mattered: they understood that trust in a marketplace is manufactured through product details (good photos, clear profiles, reviews), not just policy.

## What They Built

A straightforward web marketplace — a Ruby on Rails app — listing rooms and whole homes, with booking and payments. The deliberate early scope was a *transaction you could trust between two strangers*, in a few cities, around events that created acute demand.

## The Build

A tiny team built it themselves, with Blecharczyk handling engineering. The most famous "build" decision wasn't code at all: noticing that listings with bad photos didn't convert, the founders **rented a camera and went door-to-door in New York photographing hosts' apartments themselves** — the canonical "do things that don't scale" move. It directly lifted bookings and taught them what the marketplace actually needed.

## Design

Design was the unfair advantage. Two of three founders were RISD-trained designers, and they obsessed over the feel of listings, trust signals, and photography. Professional-quality photos became a growth lever, not a nicety.

## Go-To-Market

Early growth was hand-to-hand: targeting sold-out events, recruiting hosts in person, and the infamous **cereal-box hustle** — designing and selling "Obama O's" and "Cap'n McCain's" cereals at the 2008 Democratic National Convention to raise ~**$30K** and keep the company alive. Then **Y Combinator (Winter 2009)** gave them ~**$20K for 6%**, structure, and Paul Graham's mentorship, followed by a **Sequoia** seed.

## The Money

- **2008** — Bootstrapped, including ~$30K from selling novelty cereal.
- **Jan 2009** — **Y Combinator (W09)**: ~$20K for 6%.
- **2009** — **Sequoia Capital** seed (~$600K), then a Series A.
- Many growth rounds followed; Airbnb **IPO'd in December 2020**.

## Key Decisions

- **Do things that don't scale.** Photographing listings by hand and recruiting hosts in person seeded liquidity that ads couldn't.
- **Survive at all costs.** The cereal stunt wasn't a gimmick — it was runway that kept a now-iconic company breathing.
- **Treat trust as a product surface.** Reviews, verified profiles, and great photos were the real product in a stranger-to-stranger market.

## What To Steal

- In a marketplace, manually solve the cold-start problem in one city before automating anything.
- Founder–market fit can be aesthetic: designers built trust into a category that desperately needed it.
- Unscalable, embarrassing tactics early (cereal, door-to-door) are often exactly right — they teach you the business.
- Persistence through near-death is a feature of most great companies, not a bug.
