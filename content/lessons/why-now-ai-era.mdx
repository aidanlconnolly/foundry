Every era has a set of things that just became buildable, and the founders who win are usually the ones who saw the window open first. In 2026 the dominant shift is AI: capable models turned a huge class of previously-impossible products into weekend prototypes — and, by OECD's count, AI took **61% of global venture dollars in 2025**. That's the tailwind. It's also a trap.

## The "why now" test

A great idea answers *why this is possible or urgent today, when it wasn't before.* Three flavors of shift:

- **Technological** — capable LLMs, cheap inference, new hardware.
- **Regulatory** — a new mandate forces buyers to change (open banking, e-invoicing).
- **Behavioral** — a durable change in how people work or buy.

If you can't name a specific recent change, you may be **too early** — which, operationally, is indistinguishable from being wrong. You'll spend cash and morale waiting for a window that isn't open.

## The AI-era opportunity — and its trap

AI compresses what used to take a team months into days, and it makes entire categories (vertical agents, AI-native tools) newly winnable. But the same accessibility is the danger:

<Pull>If your only advantage is calling a model, you have no advantage — the model provider, or the next founder, can do the same thing tomorrow.</Pull>

This is **thin-wrapper risk.** As foundation models improve, they absorb features that used to be products. The defensible AI companies build a moat *around* the model:

<Framework name="Where AI-era moats actually live">
- **Proprietary data** — a data loop the model can't get elsewhere.
- **Workflow depth** — embedded so deeply in an industry's process that ripping you out is painful.
- **Distribution** — owning the customer relationship and channel.
- **Switching costs** — accumulated state, integrations, and trust.

The model is the commodity. The moat is everything you wrap around it.
</Framework>

## Living in the future

The most reliable source of "why now" ideas is simply working at the frontier of a fast-moving field until the gaps become obvious to you before they're obvious to everyone. Use the best tools daily, notice what's suddenly possible but doesn't exist yet, and build that. In 2026, "live in the future" mostly means: use frontier AI relentlessly, and pay attention to what it *almost* does.
