An MVP is not "a smaller version of the product." It's a **learning instrument** aimed at your riskiest assumption, built with the absolute minimum effort. The mistake is treating the MVP as v1 of the dream — you end up over-building to learn something a far cheaper test would have revealed. Pick the archetype that answers your scariest question for the least work.

## The four archetypes

<Framework name="MVP archetypes">
- **Landing page** — A page describing the product with a real call-to-action (sign up, pre-order, "request access"). Tests *demand and messaging* with zero product. Best when the risk is "does anyone want this?"
- **Concierge** — You deliver the service *by hand* to a handful of users, openly and manually. Tests *value and workflow* and teaches you exactly what to automate. Best when you don't yet know what the product should do.
- **Wizard of Oz** — Users think it's automated; behind the curtain, you're doing it manually. Tests *demand for the automated experience* before you build the engine.
- **Single-feature** — Build only the one feature that delivers the core value; ruthlessly cut the rest. Best when demand is clear and the risk is "can we make the core thing good enough?"
</Framework>

## Matching archetype to risk

The choice falls out of your riskiest assumption:

- Risk = *"will anyone care?"* → **landing page**. Don't build; measure intent.
- Risk = *"what does the solution even need to do?"* → **concierge**. Do it by hand and learn.
- Risk = *"will people use the automated version?"* → **Wizard of Oz**. Fake the automation.
- Risk = *"can we build the core experience well?"* → **single-feature**. Build only that.

## Do things that don't scale

The discomfort founders feel — "but this is manual and embarrassing" — is the point. Manually onboarding users, doing the work by hand, photographing listings yourself (Airbnb), DM-ing your first hundred users: these unscalable acts are how you *learn the business* and seed the first cohort. You will automate later, from a position of understanding. Trying to build the scalable system before you understand the work is how founders ship beautiful machines that do the wrong thing.

The MVP mindset, in one line: **maximize learning per unit of building.** If you can answer your scariest question without writing code, do that first.
