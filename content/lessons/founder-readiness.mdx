Most founder advice skips the unglamorous question that actually determines whether you make it: *can you personally afford — financially and psychologically — to do this for the years it will take?* The idea matters, but founder readiness is what keeps you in the game long enough for the idea to work.

## Motivation: obsession beats ambition

Startups are long and they break you repeatedly — product failures, near-death fundraises, co-founder conflict. The founders who survive are usually the ones with **genuine obsession with the problem**, not those chasing the title or a fast exit. Interrogate your own motivation: if the honest driver is "I want to be a CEO" or "I want to get rich quickly," the first hard year will be brutal. If it's "this problem genuinely bugs me and I can't not work on it," you have the fuel to outlast the dips.

## The cost of starting (the part MBAs underweight)

For a Wharton MBA or ex-consultant, the dominant cost isn't cash — it's **opportunity cost.** Several years of forgone six-figure comp, bonuses, equity, and promotion velocity is the real price tag, and it's large precisely *because* you're employable. Naming that number honestly is healthier than pretending it's zero.

<Framework name="Founder readiness checklist">
- **Motivation** — Is it obsession with the problem, or the trappings of being a founder?
- **Runway** — Personal savings ÷ monthly burn. How many months can you go with little or no income?
- **Risk tolerance** — Can you sit with 2+ years of uncertainty and possible failure?
- **Support** — Is your household/partner genuinely on board with the volatility?
- **The leap** — Do you need to go full-time now, or can you validate nights-and-weekends first?
</Framework>

## You don't have to leap blind

The romantic "quit everything on day one" story is often the *riskier* path. A more rational on-ramp: **validate cheaply while still employed** — customer interviews, a landing page, a prototype on weekends — and **stack personal runway** so that when you do go full-time, you're betting on real signal with a financial cushion. Treat your own finances like the company's: stay *default-alive* personally so a slow start doesn't force a premature quit.

Readiness isn't bravado. It's having looked clearly at the costs, found that you'd still rather do this than not, and structured the leap so you can survive long enough to win.
