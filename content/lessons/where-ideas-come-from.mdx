Good ideas rarely arrive as a thunderbolt. They accumulate from paying close attention to friction — and the founders who reliably find them have a method, not just luck.

## Problem-first beats solution-first

The most common failure mode is falling in love with a *solution* (a clever technology, an AI capability) and then hunting for a problem to attach it to. That's backwards. **Start from a problem** — an acute, frequent pain you've witnessed — and demand is far more likely to exist. Solution-first ideas produce impressive demos that nobody needs; problem-first ideas produce ugly v1s that people fight to use.

The richest problem source is your own lived friction. The annoyances you've stopped consciously noticing — the spreadsheet everyone hacks around, the process that takes five tools and three people — are gold, precisely because familiarity has made them invisible.

## Live in the future, then build what's missing

Paul Graham's framing: the best ideas come from living at the frontier of a fast-changing field and noticing what's *missing.* When you use the newest tools daily, you start to see "this should exist but doesn't" gaps before the rest of the market. In 2026 that mostly means using frontier AI relentlessly and watching for what it *almost* does — the half-step it can't quite take is often a company.

<Framework name="Three reliable idea sources">
1. **Your own scar tissue** — problems from your industry, job, or life you understand deeply (founder–market fit baked in).
2. **Living in the future** — gaps you notice by working at the technological frontier before they're obvious.
3. **Watching workarounds** — wherever people hack together duct-tape solutions, there's an unmet, validated need.
</Framework>

## Respect the toys

Many enormous companies started as something that looked like a **toy** or a trivial niche — early Twitter, Airbnb's air mattresses, a side-project dev tool. Dismissing an idea because it seems small is a classic error: the small surface often hides a large underlying shift. The right question isn't "is this big today?" but "if this works, what does it become?"

The practical takeaway: stop waiting for the perfect idea and start *collecting problems.* Keep a running log of friction, work at the frontier of your field, and pay special attention to the things you and others quietly hack around. Ideas are downstream of attention.
