Every founder eventually faces the question of liquidity — turning paper ownership into real money — and the landscape for doing so has shifted. Understanding the exit options (and the rise of secondaries) helps you build toward the outcome you actually want, because the exit you're aiming for shapes decisions years in advance.

## The classic exits

<Framework name="How founders and investors get liquidity">
- **Acquisition (M&A).** Another company buys yours for cash and/or stock. By far the most common exit, spanning everything from a small *acqui-hire* (bought mainly for the team) to a strategic mega-deal. Faster and more flexible than an IPO.
- **IPO.** Listing on a public exchange. Provides liquidity and capital and is a milestone — but it's for a small subset of companies, and brings heavy regulatory cost, scrutiny, and the quarterly-earnings treadmill.
- **Staying independent.** Not every great company sells or lists. A profitable, founder-owned company can be a wonderful outcome — liquidity comes from profits, not a sale.
</Framework>

In 2026 the exit environment is improving — the IPO window is reopening after a slow stretch — but most successful outcomes are still acquisitions, not public offerings.

## The rise of secondaries

The biggest structural change: companies **stay private far longer** than they used to. A startup founded today might not IPO or sell for a decade or more — which is a long time for founders and early employees to hold illiquid paper wealth. **Secondaries** fill that gap:

<Pull>A secondary is selling your *existing* shares to another buyer for cash — getting liquidity without the company exiting. It lets founders and employees take some money off the table years before a traditional exit.</Pull>

Secondaries (often arranged during or alongside a growth round, sometimes via dedicated platforms) have become a normal part of the modern cap-table lifecycle. They let founders de-risk personally — paying off debt, buying a home, reducing the all-or-nothing pressure — *without* having to sell or list the whole company. Used sensibly, a modest founder secondary can actually make you a *better* long-term operator by removing the desperation to exit early.

## Build toward the outcome you want

The strategic point: **the exit you want should shape how you raise and build, starting early.** If you want to stay independent, don't take growth capital that demands a huge exit. If you're building for acquisition, cultivate relationships with potential acquirers and build something strategically valuable to them. If you're aiming for an IPO, you're committing to a scale and discipline most companies never reach. None of these is "right" — but drifting into an exit by default, shaped by money you took without thinking about the endgame, is how founders end up with an outcome that serves their investors more than themselves. Decide the kind of outcome you want, and let it inform the capital you take and the company you build.
