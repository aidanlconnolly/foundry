Validation is not the warm process of confirming you're right. Done well, it's an **adversarial** process: you take the assumption most likely to kill the company and try, as cheaply as possible, to prove it false *before* you've spent a year building.

## Find the riskiest assumption

Every idea rests on a few load-bearing beliefs. From your idea scorecard, the lowest-scoring dimension is usually it — "the problem is acute," "clinics will pay for this," "we can acquire users for under $X." Name the **one belief that, if false, ends the company.** That's what you test first. Founders instinctively test the easy, comfortable assumptions; discipline means going straight at the scary one.

## Make it falsifiable

A real test can fail. "I'll show people my mockup and see if they like it" isn't a test — there's no clean way to lose. "I'll put up a pre-order page and consider it disproven if fewer than 10 of 200 visitors leave a deposit" is. Write the assumption as a statement and define, in advance, what result would make you walk away.

<Framework name="Problem before solution">
Validate in this order:
1. **Is the problem real and acute?** Evidence = existing spend, visible workarounds, commitments in Mom Test conversations.
2. **Is our specific solution wanted?** Evidence = pre-orders, signups with intent, a pilot.
3. **Can we reach these people affordably?** Evidence = a channel that actually converts.

Most founders skip step 1 and build a beautiful solution to a problem nobody urgently has.
</Framework>

## The hierarchy of evidence

Not all "validation" is equal. From weakest to strongest:

- Likes, survey "interest," compliments — **near worthless.**
- Email signups for a waitlist — weak but directional.
- Someone gives you their time, data, or an intro — **real.**
- Someone pre-pays, signs an LOI, or pilots it — **strong.**
- Someone uses it repeatedly and tells a colleague — **the gold standard.**

The single best problem signal is that people **already pay for or hack a workaround.** That proves the pain is acute today — you're not creating demand, you're redirecting it.

## Stay cheap

The whole game is learning the most per dollar and day. A landing page, a pre-sale, a concierge MVP, or five commitment-seeking conversations can disprove a bad assumption in a week. Spending three months building to "find out" is the expensive way to learn what a $0 test could have told you. Validate first; build only what survives.
