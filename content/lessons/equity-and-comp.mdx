Equity and comp decisions made casually in month one calcify into the cap table you live with for a decade. You don't need to be a lawyer, but you need the basics right — and you need to have the awkward conversations *early*, while they're cheap. (This is education, not legal or financial advice; use a startup lawyer for the real docs.)

## Founder splits

How co-founders divide equity is emotionally loaded and consequential. Principles:

- **Lean toward roughly equal for committed co-founders.** A near-even split for people taking comparable risk signals partnership and ages better than a lopsided one a co-founder quietly resents. Big imbalances (e.g. 90/10) often demotivate the junior partner exactly when you need them most.
- **Reflect commitment and risk, not just the idea.** Ideas are cheap; execution over years is the value. "It was my idea" rarely justifies a large premium.
- **Decide explicitly and early.** Drifting into ambiguity is the trap.

## Vesting protects everyone

<Framework name="Vesting basics">
- **4-year vest, 1-year cliff** is standard. You earn your equity over four years; if you leave before month 12, you get nothing (the cliff); after that it vests monthly.
- **Founders vest too** — not just employees. If a co-founder bails after three months, vesting ensures they don't walk away with a huge slice of the company.
- **Acceleration** clauses (single/double-trigger) handle what happens on acquisition — worth understanding before you sign.
</Framework>

Vesting feels distrustful to discuss, but it *protects* the committed founders. The horror story it prevents: a co-founder leaves early holding 40% fully-owned, and now your cap table — and every future fundraise and hire — is poisoned.

## The option pool

To hire, you set aside an **option pool** (commonly 10–20% of equity) to grant employees. Two things to know:

- It **dilutes existing holders** when created or expanded.
- VCs typically require you to create/expand the pool **before** their round — the "option pool shuffle" — which means *founders*, not the new investors, absorb that dilution. It effectively lowers your real pre-money. Negotiate the pool size deliberately; don't accept a needlessly large one.

## Comp early on

Founder cash comp is usually low (survival-level) until funded; you're paid in equity and ownership of the outcome. Early employees trade some cash for meaningful equity and the chance to shape the company. As you raise and grow, comp normalizes toward market. The key early discipline is preserving runway — every dollar of salary is a day off the clock.

## The one thing to do today

Have the **split + vesting conversation now**, in writing, with a lawyer for the actual paperwork (Clerky, Stripe Atlas, or counsel). The most common and most expensive founder regret is avoiding this talk because it's uncomfortable — and then living through the mess when reality forces it.
