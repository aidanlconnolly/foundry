## Snapshot

Stripe was founded in **2010** by Irish brothers **Patrick and John Collison**. It started as a developer tool to make accepting payments online trivial, and grew into the payments backbone for a huge share of the internet economy. Crucially, the Collisons weren't first-time founders fumbling in the dark — they'd already sold a previous startup, **Auctomatic**, for ~$5M in 2008 as teenagers, then enrolled at MIT and Harvard before dropping out for Stripe.

## The Origin

The insight was born from pain. As developers, the Collisons knew that accepting a credit card online in 2010 was miserable: you needed a merchant account, a gateway, a payment processor, weeks of paperwork, and arcane APIs. They started building a tool — originally "/dev/payments" — to collapse all of that. The boring, messy beginning was literally reading payment-industry documentation and cold-emailing banks for the underlying relationships no startup could easily get.

## The Insight

The pitch became legendary as **"seven lines of code."** Where integrating payments once took weeks, Stripe let a developer paste a few lines into their site and start charging cards almost immediately. They reframed payments from a *business-development problem* into a *developer-experience problem* — and developers, not procurement departments, became the buyers.

## What They Built

The v1 scope was deliberately narrow: a clean API and a few lines of JavaScript that let a web app take a card payment. They deferred almost everything an incumbent would consider essential — dashboards, fraud suites, dozens of payment methods — to nail the one thing that hurt most: *time-to-first-charge*.

## The Build

Two technical founders built the early product themselves, obsessing over API design and documentation as if they were the product (because they were). They did the unglamorous work in parallel: securing the bank and card-network relationships that made the seven lines actually settle real money. That regulatory and partnership plumbing — not the front-end — was the true moat.

## Design

Stripe's "design" was developer experience: famously beautiful docs, sensible defaults, and an API that felt like it was made by people who'd suffered the alternative. The aesthetic restraint and craft of the website and dashboard signaled trust in a category where trust is everything.

## Go-To-Market

Growth was bottoms-up and word-of-mouth among developers in San Francisco. YC's network seeded the first users; happy developers told other developers. Stripe didn't sell to CFOs — it let engineers adopt it on a side project and pull it into the company.

## The Money

- **2010** — Seed via **Y Combinator** (an estimated ~$20–30K).
- **2011** — ~**$2M** from Elon Musk, Peter Thiel, and Andreessen Horowitz; public launch in September.
- **2012** — **Sequoia Capital** and **American Express Ventures** invest at a **~$100M valuation**.
- Many large rounds followed as Stripe scaled into a global financial-infrastructure company.

## Key Decisions

- **Treat payments as a developer product, not a sales product.** This chose the entire GTM, brand, and roadmap.
- **Do the hard, invisible BD.** Locking up bank/network relationships early created a durable advantage competitors couldn't paste in seven lines.
- **Stay narrow at launch.** Solving *one* acute pain (time-to-first-charge) brilliantly beat solving ten adequately.

## What To Steal

- Find a workflow that's miserable and collapse it to near-zero effort — the "seven lines" framing is a north star for any tool.
- Make documentation and developer experience first-class; for dev tools they *are* the product.
- The flashy surface (an API) can sit on top of unglamorous, defensible plumbing (bank deals). Build both.
- Credibility compounds: prior founder track record helped the Collisons get the relationships and capital that a cold team couldn't.
