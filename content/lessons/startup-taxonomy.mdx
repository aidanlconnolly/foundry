"Startup" is a genre, not a single thing — and each sub-genre has its own physics: different customers, sales motions, capital needs, timelines, and the one metric investors fixate on. Knowing which game you're playing tells you what "good" looks like and which playbook to copy.

## The major archetypes

- **SaaS (incl. vertical SaaS).** Recurring software, usually B2B. Metric: ARR growth + net revenue retention. Vertical SaaS goes deep on one industry's workflow; horizontal serves everyone.
- **Marketplace.** Connects buyers and sellers (Airbnb, Uber). The hard part is the **cold-start**; the prize is network effects and take-rate. Metric: GMV, take rate, liquidity.
- **Consumer.** Apps and brands sold to individuals. Cheap to try, brutal to retain. Metric: DAU/MAU, retention curves, virality.
- **Fintech.** Money movement, lending, banking, payments. Heavy on trust, compliance, and unit economics. Metric: transaction volume, take rate, loss rates.
- **Dev tools / infrastructure.** Sold bottoms-up to engineers. Metric: adoption, usage, conversion to paid.
- **Deep tech / hardware / biotech.** Long R&D, big capital, regulatory gates. Metric: technical milestones before revenue.
- **Vertical AI.** Models embedded into one industry's workflow (legal, healthcare, support). The 2026 darling — domain data and workflow integration are the moat.
- **ETA / search funds.** Buy and operate an existing business instead of building one. A legitimate, lower-variance path (and a Wharton specialty via the Perlman fellowship).

## Why the label matters

The archetype determines your **default-correct decisions**: how much to raise and from whom, what your first hire should be, what traction looks like, and how long until revenue. It also tells investors instantly how to evaluate you — "we're vertical AI for X" frames every later conversation. Pick the genre consciously, then study the two or three best companies in it as your reference implementation.
