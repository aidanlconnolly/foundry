Founders are chronically wrong about timelines — almost always too optimistic about scope and too pessimistic about how fast modern tools let them move. Calibrating realistic expectations keeps you from both burning runway on a never-shipping "perfect" v1 and from over-promising investors.

## What things actually take (2026)

These are rough, real-world ranges for a *focused* software product with a builder who's heads-down:

<Framework name="Idea → traction timeline">
- **Idea → clickable prototype:** hours to days (Figma, v0).
- **Prototype → usable MVP:** a few weeks to ~2 months, with AI-assisted building.
- **MVP → first real (non-friend) users:** days to weeks after launch, *if* you've been doing discovery in parallel.
- **First users → first paying customer:** highly variable — sometimes immediate (B2B with acute pain), sometimes months (consumer).
- **Launch → early signal of product-market fit:** typically several months of iteration, not weeks.
</Framework>

The headline: **building is no longer the bottleneck.** Reaching a usable MVP in weeks is normal now. What still takes real time is *learning your way to product-market fit* — that's iteration and discovery, not typing speed.

## Why timelines blow out

The dominant culprit is **scope creep.** "We can't launch without X" — repeated ten times — turns a 3-week MVP into a 6-month epic, and the extra features rarely touch the riskiest assumption. The second culprit is *perfectionism*: polishing a product before any user has confirmed they want it. Both stem from fear of putting something imperfect in front of people.

<Pull>The MVP you're embarrassed by, shipped this month, teaches you more than the polished product you ship next year.</Pull>

## Plan in timeboxes, not scope-boxes

The fix is to fix *time* and flex *scope*, not the reverse. Decide "users will touch this in 4 weeks," then cut features until it fits. This forces the only useful question — *what's the smallest thing that tests the core bet?* — and produces learning on a schedule. Concrete, dated milestones (prototype → MVP → first paying user) beat a detailed year-long roadmap that reality will shred in week two.

## Calibrating with examples

Remember the cases: Notion's *real* timeline included years and a near-death rebuild; Figma spent four years pre-launch solving hard tech; Stripe shipped a narrow v1 fast and grew for years. The lesson isn't "everything is fast now" — it's that **the build is faster than ever, but the journey to PMF and scale is still measured in years.** Plan the build in weeks, the company in years, and don't confuse the two.
