## Snapshot

Notion was founded in **2013** by **Ivan Zhao** and **Simon Last** with the ambition of building the "LEGO of productivity tools" — letting non-engineers assemble their own software. It nearly died, was rebuilt almost from scratch in **Kyoto, Japan**, relaunched in **2016**, and reached a **$10B valuation** in 2021.

## The Origin

Zhao and Last raised about **$2M from angels** (friends, family, and friends of friends) in 2013 and set out to give ordinary people the power to make tools without code. The boring, painful truth of the early years: the first prototype was essentially a **web-page builder**, and it was *too complex for what people actually wanted.* Most people, they realized, "don't wake up wanting to build software; they wake up to get stuff done."

## The Insight

The reframing that saved the company: instead of a programmable toolkit aimed at builders, make a beautiful, flexible **docs-and-databases workspace** where the building is invisible — pages, blocks, and databases that ordinary users compose into wikis, notes, and trackers without realizing they're "building software."

## What They Built

A block-based workspace: every piece of content is a draggable block, pages nest infinitely, and databases turn the same blocks into tables, boards, and calendars. The deliberate scope was an opinionated, all-in-one tool to replace a sprawl of separate apps — notes, wiki, tasks — with one coherent surface.

## The Build

The near-death rebuild is the heart of the story. Around **2015**, facing dwindling cash and looming bankruptcy, Zhao and Last **laid off their small team, moved to Kyoto, and rewrote Notion themselves** — reportedly coding up to 18 hours a day in a modest apartment. A tiny team (roughly **4–5 people**, about **8 by version 2.0**) built what hundreds now maintain. Simon Last, the technical co-founder, wrote much of the core.

## Design

Design and craft were the differentiation in a crowded productivity market. Notion's calm aesthetic, typography, and the tactile feel of dragging blocks made an inherently complex tool feel approachable — taste as a moat.

## Go-To-Market

The **2016 relaunch won Product Hunt's #1 Product of the Day**, igniting a passionate early community. Growth was bottoms-up and community-led: power users built elaborate templates and evangelized Notion publicly, and a template/creator ecosystem became a durable acquisition channel — individuals brought it into their teams.

## The Money

- **2013** — ~**$2M** angel round.
- **2015** — Near bankruptcy; team laid off; the Kyoto rebuild.
- **2016** — Version 1.0 relaunch; Product Hunt #1.
- **Oct 2021** — **$275M** round at a **~$10B valuation**. The company grew to ~500 employees.

## Key Decisions

- **Kill your darling and rebuild.** Abandoning the over-complex first product — and the team — to rewrite it small was brutal but correct.
- **Hide the complexity.** Repositioning from "build your own software" to "get stuff done" unlocked a mass-market audience.
- **Bet on taste.** In a commoditized category, design and feel were the wedge.

## What To Steal

- Your first version being too complex is a common, survivable failure — the fix is usually *less*, reframed for what users actually want.
- A tiny, focused team can out-build a bloated one; constraints during the rebuild forced clarity.
- A passionate community and a creator/template ecosystem can become your cheapest, most durable growth engine.
