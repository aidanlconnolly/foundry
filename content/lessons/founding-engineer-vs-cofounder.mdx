How your product actually gets built — and by whom — is one of the highest-stakes early decisions, because it determines your iteration speed, your equity, and your control over the company's most important asset. There are three common configurations, and they're not interchangeable.

## The three options

<Framework name="Who builds the product">
- **Technical co-founder** — A *peer and owner*: founder-level equity (often a near-equal split), shared risk, and decision-making authority. Leads engineering and product technically. The strongest setup for most software startups.
- **Founding engineer** — An *early employee* (employee #1–5) with meaningful equity (typically low single-digit %, far below a co-founder) who does a lot of the early building. Crucial hire, but the company is not theirs.
- **Agency / contractor** — *Bought capacity.* Useful for a one-off prototype or a specific gap, but a dangerous home for your *core* product long-term.
</Framework>

## Why the distinction matters

It's not about coding skill — a founding engineer may out-code a technical co-founder. It's about **ownership, commitment, and authority.** A co-founder will fight through the 2am crises, share the dilution and the upside, and make product calls as a peer. A founding engineer is betting much less and, reasonably, expects much less. Conflating the two — promising "co-founder" while offering employee equity — is a classic, relationship-poisoning early mistake. Be precise about which role you're filling.

## The agency trap

Outsourcing the core product to an agency feels efficient when you can't build, but it usually backfires: you lose **iteration speed** (every change is a quote and a queue), you don't accumulate **product knowledge** in-house, and you don't control your **crown-jewel asset.** Agencies are fine for a marketing site, a one-off integration, or buying short-term capacity around a real team — not for owning the product that *is* your company. Investors notice, too: a startup whose product lives at an agency looks structurally fragile.

## Finding a technical co-founder

If you need one, start now — it routinely takes **months**, and it's effectively a marriage. The best sources are people you've already worked with and trust; cold "co-founder dating" works but is slower and riskier. Test the relationship on a real project before committing equity, align explicitly on vision, working style, and equity *up front*, and put **vesting** on everyone's shares so an early departure doesn't wreck the cap table.

In 2026, AI tools genuinely lower the bar — a non-technical founder can get to a real v1 alone. But as complexity and scale grow, deep technical ownership still matters. The honest question is: *can I credibly build and own this as it gets hard — and if not, who's my peer who can?*
