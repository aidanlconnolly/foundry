Before you pick an idea, get precise about what you're actually trying to build — because the word *startup* gets attached to everything from a two-person Etsy shop to OpenAI, and those are not the same animal. The distinction isn't pedantic. It decides which playbook applies: how you finance the thing, how fast you hire, how much risk is appropriate, and whether giving away half your equity to investors is brilliant or insane.

## The one-sentence definition

Paul Graham's 2012 essay settles it with unusual clarity: **"A startup is a company designed to grow fast."** Everything else people associate with startups — being newly founded, working on technology, taking venture money, aiming for an "exit" — is *common but not essential*. The only essential thing is growth.

<Pull>Being newly founded does not in itself make a company a startup. The only essential thing is growth. Everything else we associate with startups follows from it.</Pull>

That sounds almost too simple, but it has teeth. If growth is the defining property, then a company is a startup *only if it has a credible mechanism to grow fast* — a large enough market, a product people want badly, and a way to reach them that compounds. A barber shop can be excellent and profitable forever, but it cannot grow 5% a week, so it is not a startup. That's not an insult; it's a category.

## Startup vs. small business vs. SMB

These three get blurred constantly. Pulling them apart:

- **Small / lifestyle business.** Optimizes for steady income, autonomy, and longevity. A consultancy, a restaurant, a profitable niche SaaS run by two people. Growth is linear and that's fine — the goal is a good business, not a rocket.
- **SMB (the *market*, not the company).** "SMB" usually describes a *customer segment* you sell to — small and mid-sized businesses. A startup can absolutely be a fast-growing company that sells software *to* SMBs (think early Gusto or Toast).
- **Startup.** Optimizes for rapid, compounding growth, usually accepts years of losses and dilution to get there, and is betting that scale creates a durable, valuable company.

The trap for a sharp operator — especially someone arriving from consulting — is assuming "ambitious + tech + incorporated = startup." It isn't. The question is always: *is this thing built to grow fast, and is that even the right goal for what I want?*

## Why "fast" has a number

Graham puts rough numbers on it, and they're worth internalizing because they reframe how you read your own traction. For a very early startup, a good growth rate is **5–7% per week**. 10% a week is exceptional. Below ~1% a week and you probably haven't yet found something people genuinely want.

<Framework name="The growth lens">
Compounding is the whole game. 5% per week is about **12.6× per year**; 7% is roughly **33×**. That's why a startup can look like nothing for 18 months and then appear to materialize overnight — the curve was always exponential, it just started small. It also explains why startups tolerate early losses: if the growth engine is real, scale will eventually dwarf today's burn.
</Framework>

The practical move is to **pick a growth rate and steer by it.** Graham's advice to founders is almost mechanical: choose a weekly growth target, and let it force every decision. Which feature? The one that moves the number. Which customer? The one that moves the number. A growth target turns the overwhelming question "what should I do today?" into a tractable one.

## Why this is the *first* lesson

Because the rest of this curriculum — validation, building, hiring, and especially fundraising — assumes you've made an honest decision here. Venture capital, equity splits, option pools, and a 7–10 year illiquidity bet are *tools calibrated for the growth game*. Bolt them onto a lifestyle business and they'll misfire: you'll dilute yourself for capital you didn't need and chase a scale the market can't support.

So the foundational act isn't choosing an idea. It's choosing a *game*. If you want a fast-growing company, commit to the constraints that come with it. If you want a great small business, that's a legitimate and often smarter choice — it just isn't this book. Get that decision right and everything downstream gets easier; get it wrong and you'll spend years applying the wrong playbook to the right company.
