Fundraising is a process you run, not a thing that happens to you. Founders who treat it as a disciplined, time-boxed campaign — with a pipeline, a tight story, and competitive tension — raise faster and on better terms than those who email investors one at a time and hope.

## Build the pipeline

Like sales, fundraising is a funnel. Build a **target list of 30–50 investors** who genuinely fit your stage *and* sector (a seed fund for a seed, an AI-friendly fund for an AI company), and sort them into tiers. Then get to them the right way:

<Pull>Warm introductions crush cold outreach. A trusted mutual connection vouching for you is worth more than the slickest cold email. Map your network — and your investors' networks — to find the warmest path to each target.</Pull>

Cold outreach can work as a fallback, but spend your energy lining up warm intros to your top tier first. (This is a place a Wharton network is genuine leverage.)

## The deck and the data room

- **The deck** tells a tight story: the problem, your insight and "why now," the product, traction, market, team, and the ask. Its only job is to **earn the next meeting** — not to be an exhaustive document. Crisp narrative beats dense slides.
- **The data room** is an organized folder of diligence materials — metrics, cap table, financials, key contracts — ready to share the moment interest is real. Having it ready signals you're buttoned-up and speeds the close.

## Run a tight, time-boxed process

The single biggest process lever:

<Framework name="Why time-box the raise">
- **Cluster meetings** into a defined window so investors are evaluating you in parallel, not sequentially.
- **Create competitive tension** — when multiple investors move at once, offers come faster and terms improve. A term sheet in hand makes others move.
- **Protect your time and morale.** An open-ended raise that drips on for six months drains you and signals weakness ("why is this still open?").
- **Preserve optionality** — don't grab the first offer; let the process surface the best fit and terms.
</Framework>

The contrast: a *trickle* (emailing one investor, waiting two weeks, repeating) makes you look stuck and gives away all leverage. A *campaign* (open the round, take 20 meetings in three weeks, drive to term sheets) creates momentum that's visible and self-reinforcing.

## Closing

Once you have a term sheet, you negotiate (valuation *and* the terms that matter from the last lesson — preference, pool, board), confirm fit with the lead, and move through diligence to definitive docs and a wire. Pick your lead investor carefully: this is a long relationship and a board seat, not just a check. The goal isn't the highest valuation at any cost — it's the **right partner on clean terms with enough runway** to clear the next rung of the ladder.
