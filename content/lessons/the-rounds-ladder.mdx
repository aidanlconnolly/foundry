Fundraising isn't one event — it's a *ladder*, where each rung trades more proof for more money at a higher valuation. Knowing what each round actually expects keeps you from raising too early (and getting rejected) or too late (and running out of runway).

## The rungs

<Framework name="The funding ladder">
- **Pre-seed** — Funds the *search for product-market fit*: a team, a prototype, first signal. Often raised on SAFEs from angels and pre-seed funds. You're selling the team, the insight, and a sharp "why now."
- **Seed** — Funds turning early signal into a *repeatable engine*. Investors want evidence people want it (usage, retention, maybe first revenue) and a credible path to a Series A.
- **Series A** — The first big **priced** round, VC-led, with a board seat. Funds scaling a working model. The bar is real.
- **Series B and beyond** — *Growth* capital to scale a proven model: expand the team, enter new markets, accelerate. Each round expects more proof at a higher valuation.
</Framework>

## Each rung has a "prove it" bar

The ladder is fundamentally about **evidence ↔ valuation**. Climbing a rung means demonstrating you've de-risked the thing the previous round was betting on:

- Pre-seed bet: *can this team build something people want?*
- Seed bet: *is there early product-market fit and a path to repeatable growth?*
- A bet: *is the growth real, repeatable, and efficient?*
- B+ bet: *can we pour fuel on a proven engine and have it scale?*

Raise when you can credibly clear the next bar — not before. Pitching a Series A on a seed-stage story just burns reputation and time.

## The 2026 reality: a hard middle

The current market matters here (see the VC Landscape). In 2026 the market is **bifurcated** — lots of capital at the very top (frontier AI mega-rounds) and active seed funding at the bottom, but the **middle is squeezed.** Practically:

<Pull>To raise a Series A in 2026, you increasingly need to look like a former growth company — real, repeatable revenue (often ~$1M+ ARR) and efficient growth, not just a promising story.</Pull>

The implication: seed-stage founders should plan for a *higher* A bar than the playbooks of a few years ago assumed, raise enough seed runway to actually reach it, and prioritize efficient growth over vanity scale. AI-native companies that can reach revenue fast and cheaply have an advantage on this ladder.

## Match fund size to round

A practical rule woven through every rung: target investors whose **fund size fits your round.** A $1B fund won't lead your $1.5M seed; a tiny pre-seed fund can't lead your $15M A. Part of climbing the ladder well is simply pitching the right investors for the rung you're on — which is a research task as much as a pitching one.
